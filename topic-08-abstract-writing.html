Abstract Writing

Master the art of writing effective abstracts for academic and professional documents

What is Abstract Writing?

Abstract writing means a condensed form of a longer piece of writing. There are two main types of abstract that serve different purposes in academic and professional communication.

Key Purpose

Abstracts provide readers with a brief synopsis of the entire work, allowing them to quickly understand the main points and decide whether to read the full document.

Types of Abstracts

Descriptive Abstract

For publications in the humanities, social sciences, or psychology, descriptive abstracts are typically utilized. Usually, this kind of abstract is quite brief (between 50 and 100 words).

Essential Components:
  • Background
  • Purpose
  • Particular interest/focus of paper
  • Overview of contents (not always included)

Informative Abstract

Generally speaking, publications in the fields of science, engineering, or psychology use informative abstracts. You have to sum up your report's main points in no more than 200 words.

Key Components:
  • Background
  • Aim or purpose of research
  • Method used
  • Findings/results
  • Conclusion

Why do we write abstracts?

Abstracts are crucial components of research papers, reports, and even academic tasks. Usually written last, the abstract is the first thing readers see when they want a brief synopsis of the entire work.

Timing is Key

It is advised that you stop composing the abstract until the finish, as you'll have a better understanding of all your discoveries and conclusions.

How do I write an abstract?

  1. First re-read your paper/report for an overview. Then read each section and condense the information in each down to 1-2 sentences.
  2. Next read these sentences again to ensure that they cover the major points in your paper.
  3. Ensure you have written something for each of the key points outlined above for either the descriptive or informative abstract.
  4. Check the word length and further reduce your words if necessary by cutting out unnecessary words or rewriting some of the sentences into a single, more succinct sentence.
  5. Edit for flow and expression. Make sure your abstract reads smoothly and professionally.
